Evidence-based design

What users said

We ran moderated usability testing with small business owners across Australia. Their feedback directly shaped key decisions: from how we structured information to the language used throughout the journey.

🔍

Key finding

The biggest source of confusion wasn't the process; it was the terminology. Users understood what to do, but not what the words meant.

Design craft

Key Product Design Decisions

Four decisions that shaped the experience:

😵‍💫

Designing for reassurance and clarity

Users weren't always confident their actions were completed. Clear confirmation states were added to reduce uncertainty and help users move forward.

⚖️

Simplifying complex financial terminology

Users struggled to understand key banking terms, particularly the difference between borrower and guarantor. I worked with Content to simplify copy and CTA labels, balancing regulatory requirements with usability.

⏱️

Prioritising speed over completeness

Traditional lending flows were slowed by optional steps and branching paths. I worked with Product and Engineering to streamline the journey to its essentials, enabling funding in approximately 18 minutes.

🤝

Designing within technical constraints

Tight timelines required prioritising existing design system components. Where needed, I worked directly with engineers on custom solutions, maintaining quality without slowing delivery.

Looking back

Reflections & What's Next

What I Learned

  • Early alignment on scope and trade-offs is critical under extreme timelines
  • Content design plays a pivotal role in compliance-heavy experiences
  • Crisis-driven products demand greater emphasis on reassurance and clarity than standard flows

What I'd Do Next

  • Introduce eligibility pre-checks earlier in the journey to reduce drop-off
  • Use progressive disclosure for complex lending concepts to reduce cognitive load at high-stakes moments
  • Document design decisions and rationale so future teams can build on the work
